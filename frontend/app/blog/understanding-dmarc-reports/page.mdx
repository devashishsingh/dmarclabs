## What Problem Does DMARC Solve?

### Email spoofing and phishing

Without DMARC, any mail server on the internet can send an email claiming to be `you@yourdomain.com`. This is the core vector behind CEO fraud, invoice scams, and supply-chain phishing — attacks that collectively cost businesses [billions of dollars](https://www.ic3.gov/Media/PDF/AnnualReport/2023_IC3Report.pdf) annually.

DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance) is a DNS policy that instructs receiving mail servers to:

1. **Check** whether the sending IP is authorised by your SPF record
2. **Check** whether the message is signed by your DKIM key
3. **Apply a policy** (`none`, `quarantine`, or `reject`) if both checks fail
4. **Send you a report** summarising every result

> A DMARC policy at `p=reject` means that unauthenticated mail claiming to be from your domain gets dropped before it ever reaches an inbox.

### The visibility gap

Even with DMARC enforced, most teams have no clear picture of:

- Which third-party services (Mailchimp, Salesforce, Zendesk, etc.) are sending mail on their behalf
- Whether those services are properly aligned with their SPF/DKIM setup
- Whether any rogue or compromised servers are attempting to impersonate them
- Which legitimate emails are failing authentication and why

Aggregate reports answer all of these questions — but only if you can read them.

## Anatomy of a DMARC Aggregate Report

A DMARC aggregate report (sent to the address in your `rua=` DNS tag) is a gzip-compressed XML file. Here is what each section means:

### Report metadata

| Field | What it means |
|-------|--------------|
| `org_name` | The receiving mail provider who generated the report (e.g. Google, Microsoft) |
| `date_range` | The 24-hour UTC window this report covers |
| `report_id` | Unique identifier for this report |

### Policy published

This reflects what your DNS record said **at the time the report was generated**, not what it says today. Always check this first — if it does not match what you intended, a DNS change may not have propagated yet.

### Record rows

Each row represents a unique **sending IP** and contains:

| Field | What it means |
|-------|--------------|
| `source_ip` | The IP address that sent the email |
| `count` | Number of emails sent from this IP in the reporting window |
| `policy_evaluated.dkim` | Did the DKIM signature verify? (`pass` / `fail`) |
| `policy_evaluated.spf` | Was the IP in the SPF record? (`pass` / `fail`) |
| `auth_results.dkim.domain` | The domain in the DKIM `d=` tag |
| `auth_results.spf.domain` | The `MAIL FROM` (envelope sender) domain |

**DMARC alignment** requires that at least one of SPF or DKIM passes **and** the authenticated domain aligns (matches or is a subdomain of) the `From:` header domain. A pass on SPF alone is not enough if the SPF domain does not align.

## The Four Sending IP Scenarios

When you analyse a DMARC report, every sending IP falls into one of four categories. DMARC Labs shows these as **threat levels**:

### ✅ TRUSTED

A well-known legitimate sender — Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, your ESP (Mailchimp, SendGrid, Postmark), or a large verified cloud network. These IPs have a strong global reputation and the DMARC checks align correctly.

**Action:** No action needed. These are your expected senders.

### 🟡 NEUTRAL

A registered, reachable IP that is not on any blocklist but does not belong to a widely-known sender. This is common for:
- In-house mail servers
- Regional or smaller ESPs
- New cloud providers with limited reputation data

**Action:** Identify the owner in the WHOIS data. If it is a service you authorised, ensure it is correctly included in your SPF record and that DKIM is configured. If you do not recognise it, investigate further.

### 🔴 SUSPICIOUS

An IP associated with flagged Autonomous System Numbers (ASNs), open proxies, residential botnets, bulletproof hosting, or known spam/phishing infrastructure.

**Action:** If DMARC is passing for this IP, someone has incorrectly authorised it (a compromised `include:` in SPF is a common cause). If DMARC is failing, your policy is working — move to `p=reject` to block it entirely.

### ⚪ UNKNOWN

WHOIS lookups returned no usable owner information. This can mean the IP is very new, belongs to a private range, or the WHOIS server was unreachable at query time.

**Action:** Cross-reference the IP against your own send logs. Re-run the analysis at a later time if the WHOIS data was temporarily unavailable.

## Common Problems DMARC Reports Reveal

### 1. Shadow IT senders

Your marketing team signed up for a new email tool without telling IT. It is sending newsletters as `@yourdomain.com` but is not in your SPF record. DMARC reports show the IP, the volume, and the fail status — you can then either add the service to SPF or ask the team to stop using it.

### 2. SPF `~all` softfail leaking forged mail

A `~all` softfail policy means that failing mail is *marked* suspicious but still *delivered*. Reports will show these failures as policy `none` or `quarantine` outcomes. Moving to `p=reject` closes the gap.

### 3. Forwarded email breaking SPF

Forwarded email is a classic SPF failure source. When a mailbox at `user@isp.com` forwards to `user@gmail.com`, the forwarding server's IP is not in your SPF. DMARC reports will show these as SPF-fail, DKIM-pass — which is fine because DKIM still passes. This is why having DKIM configured matters.

### 4. Third-party service misconfigured

A transactional email provider is sending on your behalf, but they are using your domain in the envelope sender (SPF domain) without a CNAME/subdomain alignment setup. DMARC fails because the `From:` domain and the SPF domain do not match. The fix is either to use a subdomain (`mail.yourdomain.com`) or configure the provider's DKIM signing correctly.

### 5. Domain spoofing attempts

You see an IP from a bulletproof hosting provider sending 400 emails as your domain — all DMARC failures. These are spoofing attempts being blocked by receiving servers. Once you move to `p=reject`, these are silently discarded. You can still see them in reports, which tells you whether you are being actively targeted.

## How to Use DMARC Labs

Getting from a raw XML report to actionable intelligence takes three steps:

### Step 1 — Export your aggregate report

Log into your email admin console and download the DMARC XML report. Most providers (Google, Microsoft, Proofpoint) allow you to configure the `rua=` address to a mailbox you control. Tools like [dmarcian](https://dmarcian.com) or simply checking your mailbox will surface the `.xml.gz` files.

Save the XML file to your computer. You do not need to decompress it — **DMARC Labs accepts raw XML directly.**

### Step 2 — Drop the file into DMARC Labs

Go to [dmarclabsds1.xyz](https://www.dmarclabsds1.xyz/), drag and drop your `.xml` file onto the upload zone (or click to browse), and wait. The analysis pipeline runs entirely in-memory:

- **Memory processing** — Your file is loaded directly into the analysis engine. Nothing is written to disk.
- **Parsing DMARC XML** — Every `<record>` block is extracted and decoded into structured per-IP authentication data.
- **Enriching IPs with WHOIS** — Each unique sending IP is looked up for owner, ASN, country, and threat classification.
- **Building results** — The enriched data is aggregated into the results table.

The whole process typically completes in under 5 seconds for reports with up to 50 MB of data.

### Step 3 — Interpret the results

The results table groups every sending IP with its:

- **Email volume** — How many messages were sent from this IP in the report window
- **DMARC pass / fail counts and rate** — The authentication outcome under your policy
- **SPF pass / fail counts and rate**
- **DKIM pass / fail counts and rate**
- **Sender intelligence** — Verified owner name, country, and threat level

Sort by **Threat** (descending) to surface SUSPICIOUS IPs first. Sort by **Volume** (descending) to prioritise high-impact senders.

When you are done, click **Download CSV** to export the full enriched table. Use it in a spreadsheet, share it with your security team, or import it into your SIEM.

Your data is **never stored**. The session is purged automatically after one hour, and you can manually delete it at any time with the **Purge data** button.

## Building Toward `p=reject`

Most organisations start with `p=none` (monitoring mode) and then work toward `p=reject`. The typical path is:

1. **Set `p=none`** and collect two to four weeks of aggregate reports
2. **Identify all legitimate senders** using DMARC Labs — make sure every one has both SPF and DKIM passing and aligned
3. **Fix misconfigurations** — add missing ESPs to SPF, configure DKIM signing on each service
4. **Move to `p=quarantine`** — failing mail goes to spam rather than inbox
5. **Monitor for regressions** — re-run DMARC Labs weekly to catch any new services
6. **Move to `p=reject`** — full enforcement. Unauthenticated mail is blocked at the receiving server.

> DMARC Labs is designed to accelerate steps 1, 2, and 5. Each time you receive a new aggregate report, drop it in and you have a fresh, enriched picture of your sending infrastructure in seconds — no spreadsheet work, no manual WHOIS lookups.

It means some email claiming to be from your domain is failing authentication. The most common causes are:
- A legitimate sender not included in your SPF record
- A sender not configured to DKIM-sign with your domain
- Forwarded email breaking SPF alignment (acceptable if DKIM passes)
- Active spoofing attempts (acceptable if your policy is `p=quarantine` or `p=reject`)
